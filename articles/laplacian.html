The laplacian is a second order differential operator. When applied to the function $u$ it is denoted $\nabla^2u$ or $\Delta u$. It is the sum of the second partial derivatives with respect to each dimension. For example, for a function $u : \RR^3 \to \RR$ the laplacian is given by $$\nabla^2 u = \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} + \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y^2} + \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial z^2}.$$

Applied to Radial Basis Functions

Given a point $\vec{y} \in \RR^d$ called the center, the distance from a point $\vec{x} \in \RR^3$ to the center is given by $r = \left( \sum\limits_{i=1}^d (x_i - y_i)^2 \right)^{\frac{1}{2}}$. For a radial basis function centered at $\vec{y}$ denoted $\phi_{\vec{y}}$ we derive the laplacian as follows.

First note that for any dimension $\frac{\partial \phi_y}{\partial x_i} = \frac{d \phi_y}{d r}\frac{\partial r}{\partial x_i}$. Then \begin{align} \frac{\partial r}{\partial x_i} &= \frac{\partial}{\partial x_i} \left( \sum\limits_{i=1}^d (x_i - y_i)^2 \right)^{\frac{1}{2}} \\ &= \frac{1}{2} \left( \sum\limits_{i=1}^d (x_i - y_i)^2 \right)^{-\frac{1}{2}} 2x_i \\ & = \frac{x_i}{r} \end{align} Thus the gradient is given by $$ \nabla\phi_y = \frac{d \phi_y}{dr}\frac{1}{r} \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ \vdots \\ x_d \end{bmatrix}. $$
