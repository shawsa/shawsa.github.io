Overview

Given a set of points $\{\vec{x}_i\}_{i=1}^n$ from a surface $\SS$, and an operator $\mathcal{L}$, we would like to approximate the coressponding surface operator $\mathcal{L}_\SS$ of a function $u$ as a weighted sum of the function values $\{u(\vec{x}_i)\}_{i=1}^n$. In effect, we want the finite difference weights.

The idea is to construct an RBF interpolant $s(\vec{x})$ such that it interpolates the points, and has the property that $\mathcal{L}s(\vec{x}_i) = \mathcal{L}_\SS s(\vec{x}_i)$ for all $i=1, 2, ..., n$.

Three versions of this method are outlined here. The high order method, and low order method are from [1]. The symmetric method, is also by Dr. Piret, but has yet to be published.

Overview

The idea is to set up an orthogonal gradients interpolant as before using only points on the surface (no isosurface reconstruction). From [2] we are given the lemma that $$ \Delta_\SS f = \Delta f - \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial \vec{n}^2} - \kappa \frac{\partial f}{\partial \vec{n}} $$ where $\kappa$ is the mean curvature. For notational convenience let $\mathcal{L}_\vec{x} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial \vec{n}} = \vec{n}\cdot \nabla_\vec{x}$, and $\mathcal{H}_\vec{x} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial \vec{n}^2} = \vec{n}^TH_\vec{x}(\cdot)\vec{n} = \mathcal{L}_\vec{x}\mathcal{L}_\vec{x}$. Here $H_\vec{x}(\phi)$ denotes the hermitian with respect to $\vec{x}$ of $\phi$ and $\vec{n}$ is the outward oriented unit normal vector to the surface at $\vec{x}$. Thus if we require that at a stencil center $\vec{y}$ we have $\mathcal{H}_\vec{y}s(\vec{x}) \vert_{\vec{x}=\vec{y}} = 0$ and $\mathcal{L}_\vec{y}s(\vec{x}) \vert_{\vec{x}=\vec{y}} = 0$ we will have the key property in the Orthogonal Gradients method: $\Delta s = \Delta_\SS s$, at least at $\vec{y}$.

Using a similar method to the moment conditions, we will Let $$ s(\vec{x}) = \sum c_i \phi(||\vec{x} - \vec{x}_i||) + d \mathcal{L}_x \phi(||\vec{x} - \vec{y}||)+ e \mathcal{H}_x \phi(||\vec{x} - \vec{y}||) $$ where $\vec{y} = \vec{x}_1$ is the stencil center, and require that $s$ interpolates the surface, $\mathcal{H}_\vec{y}s(\vec{x}) \vert_{\vec{x}=\vec{y}} = 0$, and $\mathcal{L}_\vec{y}s(\vec{x}) \vert_{\vec{x}=\vec{y}} = 0$.

Then we simply calculate a closed form for $\Delta s(\vec{x})$.

Lemmas

It will be helpful to have some lemmas. These lemmas use the same notation as specified in the Overview section. Additionally we we adopt the notation that for a given radial basis function $\phi$ (or $\phi_0$), define $\phi_1(r) = \frac{1}{r}\frac{d}{dr}\phi$ and for $i>1$ define $\phi_{i+1}(r) = \frac{1}{r}\frac{d}{dr}\phi_i$.

Lemma 1

$$ \mathcal{L}_\vec{x} \phi_n(r) = \vec{n}\cdot(\vec{x}-\vec{y}) \phi_{n+1}(r) $$ Proof:

Denote $\vec{x} = \begin{bmatrix} x & y & z \end{bmatrix}^T, \vec{y} = \begin{bmatrix} a & b & c \end{bmatrix}^T$, and $r = ||\vec{x} - \vec{y}||$. Then \begin{align*} \frac{dr}{dx} &= \frac{d}{dr} \big((x-a)^2 + (y-b)^2 + (z-c)^2 \big)^{1/2} \\ &= 2(x-a)\frac{1}{2}\frac{d}{dr} \big((x-a)^2 + (y-b)^2 + (z-c)^2 \big)^{-1/2} \\ &= (x-a)\frac{1}{r} \\ \frac{d}{dx}\phi(r) &= \phi'(r)\frac{dr}{dx} = (x-a) \phi_1(r) \\ &\text{and similarly} \\ \nabla_\vec{x} \phi(r) &= (\vec{x}-\vec{y}) \phi_1(r) \\ \mathcal{L}_\vec{x}\phi(r) &= \vec{n}\cdot\nabla_\vec{x} \phi(r) = \vec{n}\cdot(\vec{x}-\vec{y}) \phi_1(r). \end{align*}

$\blacksquare$

Lemma 2

For $n \in \NN, \mathcal{L}_\vec{x}\big(\vec{n}\cdot(\vec{x}-\vec{y})\big)^{n+1} = (n+1)\big(\vec{n}\cdot(\vec{x}-\vec{y})\big)^n$.

Proof:

First denote $\vec{x} = \begin{bmatrix}x_1 & x_2 & x_3 \end{bmatrix}^T$ and $\vec{n} = \begin{bmatrix}n_1 & n_2 & n_3\end{bmatrix}$. Then \begin{align*} \frac{\partial}{\partial x_1}\big(\vec{n}\cdot(\vec{x}-\vec{y})\big)^{n+1} &= (n+1) \big(\vec{n}\cdot(\vec{x}-\vec{y})\big)^{n} n_1 \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial x_2}\big(\vec{n}\cdot(\vec{x}-\vec{y})\big)^{n+1} &= (n+1) \big(\vec{n}\cdot(\vec{x}-\vec{y})\big)^{n} n_2 \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial x_3}\big(\vec{n}\cdot(\vec{x}-\vec{y})\big)^{n+1} &= (n+1) \big(\vec{n}\cdot(\vec{x}-\vec{y})\big)^{n} n_3 \\ \nabla_\vec{x}\big(\vec{n}\cdot(\vec{x}-\vec{y})\big)^{n+1} &= (n+1) \big(\vec{n}\cdot(\vec{x}-\vec{y})\big)^{n} \vec{n} \\ \mathcal{L}_\vec{x} \big(\vec{n}\cdot(\vec{x}-\vec{y})\big)^{n+1} &= \vec{n} \cdot \nabla_\vec{x}\big(\vec{n}\cdot(\vec{x}-\vec{y})\big)^{n+1} \\ &= (n+1) \big(\vec{n}\cdot(\vec{x}-\vec{y})\big)^{n} \vec{n} \cdot \vec{n} \\ &= (n+1) \big(\vec{n}\cdot(\vec{x}-\vec{y})\big)^{n} \end{align*}

$\blacksquare$

Construct the Interpolant

We wish to form an interpolant of the form $$ s(\vec{x}) = \sum c_i \phi(||\vec{x} - \vec{x}_i||) + d \mathcal{L}_x \phi(||\vec{x} - \vec{y}||)+ e \mathcal{H}_x \phi(||\vec{x} - \vec{y}||). $$ From Lemma 1 we have that $\mathcal{L}_x \phi(r) = \vec{n}\cdot(\vec{x}-\vec{y}) \phi_1(r)$. We must also calculate \begin{align*} \mathcal{H}_\vec{x} \phi(r) &= \mathcal{L}_\vec{x}\mathcal{L}_\vec{x} \phi(r) \\ &= \mathcal{L}_\vec{x} \big( \vec{n}\cdot(\vec{x}-\vec{y}) \phi_1(r) \big) \\ &= \phi_1(r)\mathcal{L}_\vec{x} \big( \vec{n}\cdot(\vec{x}-\vec{y}) \big) + \vec{n}\cdot(\vec{x}-\vec{y}) \mathcal{L}_\vec{x} \phi_1(r) \\ &= \phi_1(r) + \big(\vec{n}\cdot(\vec{x}-\vec{y})\big)^2 \phi_2(r) \big) \\ \end{align*}

We also require that at the stencil center $\vec{y}$ we have $\mathcal{H}_\vec{y}s(\vec{x}) \vert_{\vec{x}=\vec{y}} = 0$ and $\mathcal{L}_\vec{y}s(\vec{x}) \vert_{\vec{x}=\vec{y}} = 0$. Enforcing this will require us to evaluate the following six expressions: \begin{align*} &\mathcal{L}_\vec{y} \phi(r) && \mathcal{L}_\vec{y}\mathcal{L}_\vec{x}\phi(r) && \mathcal{L}_\vec{y}\mathcal{H}_\vec{x}\phi(r) \\ &\mathcal{H}_\vec{y} \phi(r) && \mathcal{H}_\vec{y}\mathcal{L}_\vec{x}\phi(r) && \mathcal{H}_\vec{y}\mathcal{H}_\vec{x}\phi(r). \end{align*} From above we have that \begin{align*} \mathcal{L}_\vec{y}\phi(r) &= \vec{n}\cdot(\vec{y}-\vec{x}) \phi_1(r) \\ &= -\mathcal{L}_\vec{x}\phi(r) \\ \mathcal{H}_\vec{y} \phi(r) &= \phi_1(r) + \big(\vec{n}\cdot(\vec{y}-\vec{x})\big)^2 \phi_2(r) \big) \\ &= \mathcal{H}_\vec{x} \phi(r) \\ \end{align*} from which it follows that \begin{align*} \mathcal{L}_\vec{y}\mathcal{L}_\vec{x}\phi(r) &= -\mathcal{H}_\vec{x}\phi(r) \\ \mathcal{L}_\vec{y}\mathcal{H}_\vec{x}\phi(r) &= -\mathcal{L}_\vec{x}\mathcal{H}_\vec{x}\phi(r) \\ \mathcal{H}_\vec{y}\mathcal{L}_\vec{x}\phi(r) &= \mathcal{H}_\vec{x}\mathcal{L}_\vec{x}\phi(r) = \mathcal{L}_\vec{x}^3 = \mathcal{L}_\vec{x}\mathcal{H}_\vec{x}\phi(r) \\ \mathcal{H}_\vec{y}\mathcal{H}_\vec{x}\phi(r) &= \mathcal{H}_\vec{x}\mathcal{H}_\vec{x}\phi(r). \end{align*} and thus we need only calculate two expressions: $$ \mathcal{L}_\vec{x}\mathcal{H}_\vec{x}\phi(r) \phantom{=} \text{ and } \phantom{=} \mathcal{H}_\vec{x}\mathcal{H}_\vec{x}\phi(r). $$

We next observe that \begin{align*} \mathcal{L}_\vec{x}\mathcal{H}_\vec{x}\phi(r) &= \mathcal{L}_\vec{x} \bigg( \phi_1(r) + \big(\vec{n}\cdot(\vec{x}-\vec{y})\big)^2 \phi_2(r) \big) \bigg) \\ &= \mathcal{L}_\vec{x} \phi_1(r) + \phi_2(r)\mathcal{L}_\vec{x}\big(\vec{n}\cdot(\vec{x}-\vec{y})\big)^2 + \big(\vec{n}\cdot(\vec{x}-\vec{y})\big)^2 \mathcal{L}_\vec{x} \phi_2(r) \\ &= \vec{n}\cdot(\vec{x}-\vec{y})\phi_2(r) + 2\vec{n} \cdot (\vec{x}-\vec{y}) \phi_2(r) + \big(\vec{n}\cdot(\vec{x}-\vec{y})\big)^3 \phi_3(r) \\ &= 3\vec{n} \cdot (\vec{x}-\vec{y}) \phi_2(r) + \big(\vec{n}\cdot(\vec{x}-\vec{y})\big)^3 \phi_3(r) \\ \mathcal{H}_\vec{x}\mathcal{H}_\vec{x} \phi(r) &= \mathcal{L}_\vec{x}\mathcal{L}_\vec{x}\mathcal{H}_\vec{x} \\ &= \mathcal{L}_\vec{x} \bigg( 3\vec{n} \cdot (\vec{x}-\vec{y}) \phi_2(r) + \big(\vec{n}\cdot(\vec{x}-\vec{y})\big)^3 \phi_3(r) \bigg) \\ &= 3\phi_2(r)\mathcal{L}_\vec{x}\big(\vec{n} \cdot (\vec{x}-\vec{y})\big) + 3\vec{n} \cdot (\vec{x}-\vec{y})\mathcal{L}_\vec{x}\phi_2(r) + \\ &\phantom{=====} \phi_3(r) \mathcal{L}_\vec{x} \big(\vec{n}\cdot(\vec{x}-\vec{y})\big)^3 + \big(\vec{n}\cdot(\vec{x}-\vec{y})\big)^3 \mathcal{L}_\vec{x} \phi_3(r) \\ &= 3\phi_2(r) + 3\big(\vec{n} \cdot (\vec{x}-\vec{y})\big)^2\phi_3(r) + \\ &\phantom{=====} 3\big(\vec{n} \cdot (\vec{x}-\vec{y})\big)^2\phi_3(r) + \big(\vec{n}\cdot(\vec{x}-\vec{y})\big)^4 \phi_4(r) \\ &= 3\phi_2(r) + 6\big(\vec{n} \cdot (\vec{x}-\vec{y})\big)^2\phi_3(r) + \big(\vec{n}\cdot(\vec{x}-\vec{y})\big)^4 \phi_4(r) \end{align*}

Finally we have our interpolation matrix equation $$ \begin{bmatrix} \phi(|| \vec{x}_i-\vec{x}_j ||) & \mathcal{L}_\vec{x}\phi(|| \vec{x}-\vec{x}_1 ||)\vert_{\vec{x} = \vec{x}_i} & \mathcal{H}_\vec{x}\phi(|| \vec{x}-\vec{x}_1 ||)\vert_{\vec{x} = \vec{x}_i} \\ -\mathcal{L}_\vec{x}\phi(|| \vec{x}-\vec{x}_1 ||)\vert_{\vec{x} = \vec{x}_j} & -\mathcal{H}_\vec{x}\phi(|| \vec{x}-\vec{x}_1 ||)\vert_{\vec{x} = \vec{x}_1} & -\mathcal{L}_\vec{x}\mathcal{H}_\vec{x}\phi(|| \vec{x}-\vec{x}_1 ||)\vert_{\vec{x} = \vec{x}_1} \\ \mathcal{H}_\vec{x}\phi(|| \vec{x}-\vec{x}_1 ||)\vert_{\vec{x} = \vec{x}_j} & \mathcal{L}_\vec{x}\mathcal{H}_\vec{x}\phi(|| \vec{x}-\vec{x}_1 ||) \vert_{\vec{x} = \vec{x}_1} & \mathcal{H}_\vec{x}\mathcal{H}_\vec{x}\phi(r) \vert_{\vec{x} = \vec{x}_1} \\ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \vec{c} \\ d \\ e \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \vec{f} \\ 0 \\0 \end{bmatrix} $$
