Stability Analysis in the traveling front case.

Traveling Wave Solution

The traveling wave solution listed in report 2022-08-08 is not comprehensive. We have identified (through simulation) a separate traveling front with a negative wave speed in the large $\tau_q$ regime. Here, we derive the traveling front solution again, and emphasize the mistake in the earlier derivation.

For reference, the model is \begin{align*} \mu u_t &= -u + w \ast (q f[u]) \\ \alpha q_t &= 1 - q - \beta q f[u]. \end{align*} In characteristic coordinates $\xi = x - c t$ this becomes \begin{align*} \mu u_t - c \mu u_\xi &= -u + w \ast (q f[u]) \\ \alpha q_t - c \alpha q_\xi &= 1 - q - \beta q f[u]. \end{align*}

We seek a traveling front. To this end, we assume that the activity variable $u$ crosses the threshold exactly once. Any translate of the traveling front solution will also be a solution, so without loss of generality, we will assume that the threshold crossing occurs at $\xi = 0$. Thus we assume the active region is given by $H(-\xi)$.

Assuming the solution is fixed in characteristic coordinates $u(\xi, t) = U(\xi)$, $q(\xi, t) = Q(\xi)$, we arrive at \begin{align*} -c\mu U' &= -U + \int_{-\infty}^0 \frac{1}{2} e^{-|\xi - \xi'|} Q(\xi') \ d\xi' \\ -c \alpha Q' &= 1 - Q - \beta Q H(-\xi). \end{align*}

The form of this equation motivates division by $c$, and so we first consider the case $c = 0$ separately.

Our system simplifies to an integral system \begin{align*} 0 &= -U + \int_{-\infty}^0 \frac{1}{2} e^{-|\xi - \xi'|} Q(\xi') \ d\xi' \\ 0 &= 1 - Q - \beta Q H(-\xi). \end{align*} Solving the second equation algebraically gives the piecewise constant equation \begin{align*} Q &= \frac{1}{1 + \beta H(-\xi)} = \begin{cases} 1, & \xi > 0 \\ \gamma, & \xi < 0 . \end{cases} \end{align*} Recall that $\gamma = \frac{1}{1+\beta}$. Substituting, we find \begin{align*} U &= \int_{-\infty}^0 \frac{1}{2} e^{-|\xi - \xi'|} \gamma \ d\xi' \\ &= \frac{\gamma}{2} \int_{-\infty}^0 e^{-|\xi - \xi'|} \ d\xi' \\ &= \frac{\gamma}{2} \begin{cases} \int_{-\infty}^0 e^{-\xi} e^{\xi'} \ d\xi', & \xi > 0 \\ \int_{-\infty}^\xi e^{-\xi} e^{\xi'} \ d\xi' + \int_{\xi}^0 e^{\xi} e^{-\xi'} d \xi', & \xi < 0 \end{cases} \\ &= \frac{\gamma}{2} \begin{cases} e^{-\xi}, & \xi > 0 \\ e^{-\xi}(e^\xi - 0) + e^{\xi}(-1 + e^{-\xi}), & \xi < 0 \end{cases} \\ &= \frac{\gamma}{2} \begin{cases} e^{-\xi}, & \xi > 0 \\ 2 - e^{\xi}, & \xi < 0 \end{cases} \\ &= \begin{cases} \frac{\gamma}{2} e^{-\xi}, & \xi > 0 \\ \gamma - \frac{\gamma}{2} e^{\xi}, & \xi < 0. \end{cases} \end{align*} Enforcing consistency gives $\theta = U(0) = \frac{\gamma}{2}$ and we see that $c = 0$ is only possible for the very specific parameter choice $$ 2 \theta = \gamma. $$

Assuming $c \ne 0$ we have \begin{align*} U' - \frac{1}{c\mu} U &= -\frac{1}{2c\mu} \int_{-\infty}^0 e^{-|-\xi - \xi'|} Q(\xi') \ d\xi' \\ Q' - \frac{1 + \beta H(-\xi)}{c\alpha}Q &= - \frac{1}{c\alpha}. \end{align*} Note that the second equation is independent of $U$, and has piecewise constant coefficients. Letting $r_\xi = -\frac{1 + \beta H(-\xi)}{c \alpha}$ we solve \begin{align*} Q' + r_\xi Q &= -\frac{1}{c\alpha} \\ \big[ Q e^{r_\xi \xi} \big]' &= -\frac{1}{c\alpha} e^{r_\xi \xi} \\ Q e^{r_\xi \xi} &= Q_0 + -\frac{1}{c\alpha}\int_{0}^\xi e^{r_\xi \xi'} \ d\xi' \\ &= Q_0 - \frac{1}{c\alpha r_\xi} \big( e^{r_\xi \xi} - 1 \big) \\ Qe^{r_\xi \xi} &= \left( Q_0 + \frac{1}{c \alpha r_\xi} \right) - \frac{1}{c\alpha r_\xi} e^{r_\xi \xi} \\ Q &= \left( Q_0 + \frac{1}{c \alpha r_\xi} \right)e^{-r_\xi \xi} - \frac{1}{c\alpha r_\xi} \\ Q &= \begin{cases} (Q_0 - 1)e^{\xi/c \alpha} + 1, & \xi > 0 \\ (Q_0 - \gamma)e^{\xi/c\alpha\gamma} + \gamma , & \xi < 0. \end{cases} \end{align*}

Here we see the crucial error. Previously, we argued that $e^{\xi/c\alpha}$ grows without bound on $\xi > 0$ and thus $Q_0 = 1$. This is only true if $c > 0$. If the converse is true, we must enforce boundedness on the other piece and conclude that $Q_0 = \gamma$. We will first consider this new case.

We have \begin{align*} Q(\xi) &= \begin{cases} 1 + (\gamma - 1) e^{\xi / c\alpha}, & \xi > 0 \\ \gamma, & \xi < 0 \end{cases} \\ U' - \frac{1}{c\mu} U &= -\frac{\gamma}{2c \mu} \int_{-\infty}^0 e^{-|\xi - \xi'|} \ d\xi' \\ &= -\frac{\gamma}{2c \mu} \begin{cases} e^{-\xi} \int_{-\infty}^0 e^{\xi'} d \xi', & \xi > 0 \\ e^{-\xi} \int_{-\infty}^\xi e^{\xi'} \ d\xi' + e^{\xi} \int_{\xi}^0 e^{-\xi'} \ d\xi' , & \xi < 0 \end{cases} \\ &= -\frac{\gamma}{2c \mu} \begin{cases} e^{-\xi} , & \xi > 0 \\ 2 - e^{\xi}, & \xi< 0 \end{cases} \\ \big[ U e^{-\xi/c\mu} \big]' &= -\frac{\gamma}{2c \mu} \begin{cases} e^{-\frac{1 + c\mu}{c\mu} \xi} , & \xi > 0 \\ 2e^{-\xi/c\mu} - e^{-\frac{1 - c\mu}{c\mu}\xi}, & \xi< 0 \end{cases} \\ U e^{-\xi/c\mu} &= U_0 -\frac{\gamma}{2c \mu} \begin{cases} \int_{0}^\xi e^{-\frac{1 + c\mu}{c\mu} \xi'} \ d\xi', & \xi > 0 \\ \int_0^\xi 2e^{-\xi'/c\mu} - e^{-\frac{1 - c\mu}{c\mu}\xi'} \ d\xi', & \xi< 0 \end{cases} \\ &= U_0 -\frac{\gamma}{2c \mu} \begin{cases} -\frac{c\mu}{1+c\mu}\left(e^{-\frac{1 + c\mu}{c\mu} \xi} - 1\right), & \xi > 0 \\ -2c\mu \left(e^{-\xi/c\mu} - 1\right) + \frac{c\mu}{1 - c\mu}\left( e^{-\frac{1 - c\mu}{c\mu}\xi} - 1 \right), & \xi< 0 \end{cases} \\ &= U_0 -\frac{\gamma}{2} \begin{cases} -\frac{1}{1+c\mu}\left(e^{-\frac{1 + c\mu}{c\mu} \xi} - 1\right), & \xi > 0 \\ 2 - \frac{1}{1-c\mu} -2 e^{-\xi/c\mu} + \frac{1}{1 - c\mu} e^{-\frac{1 - c\mu}{c\mu}\xi}, & \xi< 0 \end{cases} \\ U(\xi) &= \begin{cases} \left(U_0 - \frac{\gamma}{2(1+c\mu)} \right)e^{\xi/c\mu} + \frac{\gamma}{2(1+c\mu)}e^{-\xi}, & \xi > 0 \\ \left(U_0 - \gamma + \frac{\gamma}{2(1-c\mu)} \right)e^{\xi/c\mu} + \gamma - \frac{\gamma}{2(1-c\mu)}e^{\xi}, & \xi < 0. \end{cases} \end{align*}

Since $c < 0$ this is bounded for the $\xi > 0$ piece, but for it to remain bounded in $\xi < 0$ we require \begin{align*} 0 &= U_0 - \gamma + \frac{\gamma}{2(1-c\mu)} \\ \gamma - \theta &= \frac{\gamma}{2(1-c\mu)} \\ 1 - c\mu &= \frac{\gamma}{2(\gamma - \theta)} \\ 1 - \frac{\gamma}{2(\gamma - \theta)} &= c \mu \\ \frac{\gamma - 2\theta}{2(\gamma - \theta)} &= c \mu \\ c &= \frac{1}{2\mu} \left(1 - \frac{\theta}{\gamma - \theta} \right). \end{align*}

We require $\gamma < 2 \theta$ for the sign of $c$ to be consistent. Since we also require $\gamma > \theta$ for the front to exist, we conclude that regressive fronts only occur when $$ \theta < \gamma < 2\theta. $$

Thus, regressive fronts only occur when $\theta < \gamma < 2\theta$ and have the form \begin{align*} U(\xi) &= \begin{cases} \left(\theta - \frac{\gamma}{2(1+c\mu)} \right)e^{\xi/c\mu} + \frac{\gamma}{2(1+c\mu)}e^{-\xi}, & \xi > 0 \\ \gamma - \frac{\gamma}{2(1-c\mu)}e^{\xi}, & \xi < 0. \end{cases} \\ Q(\xi) &= \begin{cases} 1 + (\gamma - 1) e^{\xi / c\alpha}, & \xi > 0 \\ \gamma, & \xi < 0 \end{cases} \end{align*} with speed given by $$ c = \frac{1}{2\mu} \left( 1 - \frac{\theta}{\gamma - \theta}\right). $$

We have \begin{align*} Q(\xi) &= \begin{cases} 1, & \xi > 0 \\ \gamma + (1- \gamma)e^{\xi/c\alpha\gamma}, & \xi < 0 \end{cases} \\ -c\mu U' + U &= \frac{1}{2} \int_{-\infty}^0 e^{-|\xi - \xi'|} \bigg[ \gamma + (1-\gamma) e^{\xi'/c\alpha\gamma} \bigg] \ d \xi' \\ U' - \frac{1}{c\mu} U &= -\frac{1}{2c\mu} \int_{-\infty}^0 e^{-|\xi - \xi'|} \bigg[ \gamma + (1-\gamma) e^{\xi'/c\alpha\gamma} \bigg] \ d \xi'. \end{align*}

Suppose $\xi > 0$. Then \begin{align*} U' - \frac{1}{c\mu} U &= -\frac{1}{2 c \mu} e^{-\xi} \int_{-\infty}^0 \gamma e^{\xi'} + (1-\gamma) e^{\frac{1+c\alpha \gamma}{c\alpha \gamma} \xi'} \ d \xi' \\ &= -\frac{1}{2c\mu}e^{-\xi} \bigg[ \gamma + \frac{c\alpha\gamma(1-\gamma)}{1 + c\alpha\gamma} \bigg] \\ \big[U e^{-\xi/c\mu} \big]' &= - \frac{\gamma + c\alpha\gamma}{2c\mu(1+c\alpha\gamma)} e^{-\frac{1+c\mu}{c\mu} \xi} \\ U e^{-\xi/c\mu} &= U_0 - \frac{\gamma + c\alpha\gamma}{2c\mu(1+c\alpha\gamma)} \int_0^\xi e^{-\frac{1+c\mu}{c\mu} \xi'} \ d\xi' \\ &=U_0 + \frac{\gamma + c\alpha\gamma}{2(1+c\alpha\gamma)(1+c\mu)} \big( e^{-\frac{1+c\mu}{c\mu} \xi} - 1\big) \\ U(\xi) &= \left( U_0 - \frac{\gamma + c\alpha\gamma}{2(1+c\alpha\gamma)(1+c\mu)} \right) e^{\xi/c\mu} + \frac{\gamma + c\alpha\gamma}{2(1+c\alpha\gamma)(1+c\mu)} e^{-\xi} \end{align*}

Suppose that $\xi < 0$. Then \begin{align*} U' - \frac{1}{c\mu}U &= -\frac{1}{2c\mu} \int_{-\infty}^0 e^{-|\xi - \xi'|} \bigg( \gamma + (1-\gamma)e^{\xi' / c\alpha \gamma} \bigg) \ d\xi' \\ &= -\frac{1}{2c\mu} \bigg[ e^{-\xi} \int_{-\infty}^\xi \gamma e^{\xi'} + (1-\gamma) e^{\frac{1+c\alpha\gamma}{c\alpha\gamma} \xi'} \ d\xi' + e^{\xi} \int_{\xi}^0 \gamma e^{-\xi'} + (1-\gamma) e^{\frac{1-c\alpha\gamma}{c\alpha\gamma} \xi'} \ d\xi' \bigg] \\ &= -\frac{1}{2c\mu} \bigg[ e^{-\xi} \bigg(\gamma e^{\xi} + \frac{(1-\gamma)c\alpha\gamma}{1+c\alpha\gamma} e^{\frac{1+c\alpha\gamma}{c\alpha\gamma} \xi} \bigg) + e^{\xi}\bigg( -\gamma + \gamma (e^{-\xi}) + \frac{(1-\gamma)c\alpha\gamma}{1-c\alpha\gamma} \left( 1 - e^{\frac{1-c\alpha\gamma}{c\alpha\gamma} \xi}\right) \bigg)\bigg] \\ &= -\frac{\gamma}{c\mu} - \frac{1}{2c\mu} \left( \frac{(1-\gamma)c\alpha\gamma}{1+c\alpha\gamma} - \frac{(1-\gamma)c\alpha\gamma}{1-c\alpha\gamma} \right) e^{\xi/c\alpha\gamma} -\frac{1}{2c\mu} \bigg(\frac{(1-\gamma)c\alpha\gamma}{1-c\alpha\gamma} - \gamma \bigg) e^{\xi} \\ &= -\frac{\gamma}{c\mu} \underbrace{-\frac{(1-\gamma)c\alpha^2\gamma^2}{\mu(1 - c^2\alpha^2\gamma^2)}}_{K_1} e^{\xi/c\alpha\gamma} \underbrace{-\frac{1}{2c\mu} \bigg(\frac{(1-\gamma)c\alpha\gamma}{1-c\alpha\gamma} - \gamma \bigg)}_{K_2} e^{\xi} \\ [Ue^{-\xi/c\mu}]' &= -\frac{\gamma}{c\mu}e^{-\xi/c\mu} + K_1 e^{r\xi} + K_2 e^{\frac{c\mu - 1}{c\mu}\xi} \tag{$r = \frac{1}{c\alpha \gamma} - \frac{1}{c\mu}$} \\ Ue^{-\xi/c\mu} &= U_0 + \gamma(e^{-\xi/c\mu} - 1) + \frac{K_1}{r}(e^{r\xi} - 1) + \frac{K_2 c\mu}{c\mu - 1} (e^{\frac{c\mu-1}{c\mu}} - 1) \\ U(\xi) &= \bigg(U_0 - \gamma - \frac{K_1}{r} - \frac{K_2c\mu}{c\mu - 1} \bigg) e^{\xi/c\mu} + \gamma + \frac{K_1}{r}e^{\xi/c\alpha\gamma} + \frac{K_2c\mu}{c\mu-1}e^{\xi}. \end{align*} We see that the $\xi<0$ piece is bounded for $c > 0$ regardless of the other parameters. However, enforcing boundedness on the $\xi > 0$ piece, gives \[ \theta = \frac{\gamma + c\alpha\gamma}{2(1+c\alpha\gamma)(1+c\mu)}. \]

Bifurcation

From Report 2022-08-08 we have the wave speed $c$ given implicitly by $$ \theta = \frac{\alpha c + 1}{2(\mu c + 1)( 1 + \beta + \alpha c)} $$ in the traveling front case (note the change $\alpha\beta \mapsto \beta$). This gives a quadratic equation in $c$ and thus we expect up to two values for the wave speed for any given parameter regime. Fig 1 shows both branches of the wave speed as the parameter $\tau_q = \alpha$ varies, for several values of $\gamma = \frac{1}{1+\beta}$.

Numerical experimentation (for example Report 2022-08-08) suggests that the solid curves in Figure 1 represent stable traveling waves. Presumably, the dashed lines represent unstable traveling waves, with a bifurcation where the two meet. To verify this, we will attempt a linear stability analysis. In characteristic coordinates we have \begin{align*} -c\tau_u u_\xi + \tau_u u_t &= -u + \int_\RR w(\xi, y) q(y, t) f[u(y,t)] \ dy \\ -c \tau_q q_\xi + \tau_q q_t &= 1 - q - \beta q f[u] \end{align*} Let our solution be a perturbation of the traveling front solution \begin{align*} u(\xi, t) &= U(\xi) + \varepsilon \phi(\xi, t) \\ q(\xi, t) &= Q(\xi) + \varepsilon \psi(\xi, t) \end{align*} Substituting, we have \begin{align*} -c\tau_u (U' +\varepsilon \phi_\xi) + \tau_u \varepsilon \phi_t &= -U - \varepsilon \phi + \int_\RR w(\xi, y)\big(Q(y) + \varepsilon \psi(y, t)\big) f\big[U(y) + \varepsilon \phi(y, t) \big] \ dy \\ -c \tau_q (Q' + \varepsilon \psi_\xi) + \tau_q \varepsilon \psi_t &= 1 - Q - \varepsilon \psi - \beta(Q + \varepsilon \psi)f\big[U + \varepsilon \phi \big] \end{align*} Linearizing and collecting $\OO(1)$ terms, we have \begin{align*} -c\tau_u U' &= -U + \int_\RR w(\xi, y)Q(y)f[U(y)] \ dy \\ -c \tau_q Q' &= 1 - Q - \beta Qf[U] \end{align*} which are satisfied, since $U, Q$ are the traveling front solution. Collecting $\OO(\varepsilon)$ terms, we have \begin{align*} \tau_u \phi_t &= c\tau_u \phi_\xi -\phi + \int_\RR w(\xi, y)\bigg( f[U(y)] \psi + Q(y)f'[U(y)] \phi \bigg) \ dy \\ \tau_q \psi_t &= c\tau_q \psi_\xi -\psi - \beta(f[U] \psi + Qf'[U]\phi). \end{align*} Assume that the solution is separable, i.e. \begin{align*} \begin{bmatrix} \phi(\xi, t) \\ \psi(\xi, t) \end{bmatrix} &= g(t) \begin{bmatrix} \phi_0(\xi) \\ \psi_0(\xi) \end{bmatrix}. \end{align*} Substituting and isolating the separated functions, we have \begin{align*} \frac{g'(t)}{g(t)} &= \lambda = \frac{c\tau_u \phi_0' - \phi_0 + \int_\RR w(\xi, y)\bigg( f[U(y)] \psi_0 + Q(y)f'[U(y)] \phi_0 \bigg) \ dy}{\tau_u \phi_0} \\ \frac{g'(t)}{g(t)} &= \lambda = \frac{c\tau_q \psi_0' - \psi_0 - \beta(f[U] \psi_0 + Qf'[U]\phi_0)}{\tau_q \psi_0}. \end{align*} This gives $g(t) = e^{\lambda t}$ and the following spatial system \begin{align*} -c\tau_u \phi_0' &= -(1 + \tau_u \lambda)\phi_0 + \int_\RR w(\xi, y)\bigg( f[U(y)] \psi_0 + Q(y)f'[U(y)] \phi_0 \bigg) \ dy \tag{Eig u} \label{eqn:eig_u}\\ -c\tau_q \psi_0' &= - (1 + \tau_q \lambda)\psi_0 - \beta(f[U] \psi_0 + Qf'[U]\phi_0). \tag{Eig q} \label{eqn:eig_q} \end{align*} Finally, we conclude that linear stability can be characterized by the spectrum of the operator \begin{align*} \LL \begin{bmatrix}\phi_0 \\ \psi_0 \end{bmatrix} &= \begin{bmatrix} c \phi_0' - \frac{1}{\tau_u} \phi_0 + w \ast (f'[U]Q \phi_0 + f[U]\psi_0) \\ c \psi_0' - \frac{1}{\tau_q} \psi_0 - \frac{\beta}{\tau_q} (f'[U]Q \phi_0 + f[U]\psi_0) \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}

Here, we make two observations. First note, that this operator reduces to an integral operator when $c = 0$. In fact, $Q$ is no longer continuous, and the naive linearization results in the expression $Q(y)f'[U(y)] = \frac{Q(0)}{|U'(0)|}\delta(y)$ being ill-posed. We also suspect that the intersection of the two branches in the case $\gamma = 0.2$ to be a transcritical bifurcation. This motivates us to restrict ourselves to the $c > 0$ case, for the time being, to reduce the amount of book-keeping and notational complexity that we expect to arise (for example: when working with inequalities involving $c$ that come from restricting the real part of an exponential rate to have a certain sign).

Secondly, to rigorously address the spectrum of this operator, we must choose a domain. This turns out to be non-trivial. Many choices of $\phi_0$ will introduce delta distributions in the second component from $f'[U] Q \phi_0$ and thus the co-domain of the second component ought to be the Schwartz space $S'(\RR)$. However, the term $f[U] \psi_0$ is not well posed when $\psi_0$ is a delta distribution, thus we cannot choose $S'(\RR)$ to be the domain of the second component. Whether we take each component of the domain to be $C^\infty(\RR)$, $L^2(\RR)$, or $S'(\RR)$, we reasonably exclude functions that have larger than polynomial growth in the $\pm \infty$ limit. This may be enough, to characterize the spectrum

Point Spectrum

We seek eigenvalues $\lambda \in \CC$ and corresponding eigenfunctions $[\phi \quad \psi]^T$ that satisfy the system \begin{align*} \lambda \begin{bmatrix}\phi \\ \psi \end{bmatrix} &= \begin{bmatrix} c \phi' - \frac{1}{\tau_u} \phi + w \ast (f'[U]Q \phi + f[U]\psi) \\ c \psi' - \frac{1}{\tau_q} \psi - \frac{\beta}{\tau_q} (f'[U]Q \phi + f[U]\psi) \end{bmatrix}. \end{align*} (Note that we have dropped the subscript. Here, $\phi, \psi: \RR \to \CC$ are functions of a single variable, previously denoted $\phi_0$ and $\psi_0$.)

We can simplify this somewhat by noting that $f'[U]Q\phi = \frac{1}{\theta}\phi_0 \delta(\xi)$ where $\phi_0 = \phi(0)$. We will also use the notation $\mu = \tau_u$ and $\alpha = \tau_q$ for the time being. We also define the following intermediate variables $$\begin{align*} s &= -\frac{1+\mu\lambda}{c\mu} & r_+ &= -\frac{1 + \alpha \lambda}{c \alpha} & r &= -\frac{1+\beta + \alpha \lambda}{c \alpha}. \end{align*}$$

We seek a solution to the following system $$\begin{align*} 0 &= \phi' - \frac{1 + \mu \lambda}{c \mu} \phi + \frac{1}{c} w \ast\big( f'[U]Q \phi + f[U] \psi \big) \\ 0 &= \psi' - \frac{1 + \beta f[U] + \alpha \lambda}{c \alpha} \psi - \frac{\beta}{\alpha c}\big(f'[U] Q\phi + f[U] \psi \big) \end{align*}$$

The second equation can be solved piecewise. For $\xi > 0$ we have $$\begin{align*} \psi' + r_+ \psi &= 0 \\ \psi(\xi) &= \psi_+ e^{-r_+\xi} \end{align*}$$ for some constant $\psi_+$. Here, we require $\psi_+ = 0$ or $\Re(r_+) \ge 0$ else the solution has larger than polynomial growth.

For $\xi < 0$ we have $$\begin{align*} \psi' + r \psi &= 0 \\ \psi(\xi) &= \psi_- e^{-r\xi} \end{align*}$$ for some constant $\psi_-$. Here, we require $\psi_- = 0$ or $\Re(r) \le 0$ else the solution has larger than polynomial growth.

Finally, these constants obey the jump condition $$ \psi_+ - \psi_- = - \phi_0\frac{\beta}{c\alpha\theta}. $$

These restrictions lead to the following four cases

  • $\psi_+ \ne 0 \ne \psi_-$ and both $\Re(r_+) \ge 0$ and $\Re(r) \le 0$
  • $\psi_- = \psi_+ = 0$
  • $\psi_- = 0$ and $\Re(r_+) \ge 0$
  • $\psi_+ = 0 \implies \psi_- = \phi_0 \frac{\beta}{c\alpha\theta}$ and $\Re(r) \le 0$

In the process of solving for $\phi$, several more cases arise. We outline the calculation for the general case, note the exceptions, and handle them individually below.

The General Case

Substituting $\psi$ into the first equation gives $$\begin{align*} \phi' + s\phi &= -\frac{1}{2c\theta}\phi_0 e^{-|\xi|} - \frac{1}{2c}\psi_- \int_{-\infty}^0 e^{-|\xi - \xi'|} e^{-r\xi'} \ d\xi'. \end{align*}$$ We again solve this piecewise.

For $\xi > 0$ we have \begin{align*} \phi' + s\phi &= -\frac{1}{2c\theta}\phi_0 e^{-\xi} - \frac{1}{2c}\psi_- e^{-\xi}\int_{-\infty}^0 e^{(1-r)\xi'} \ d\xi' \\ &= -\frac{1}{2c\theta}\phi_0 e^{-\xi} - \frac{1}{2c(1-r)}\psi_- e^{-\xi} \\ &= \bigg(-\frac{1}{2c\theta}\phi_0 - \frac{1}{2c(1-r)}\psi_- \bigg)e^{-\xi} \\ \phi(\xi) &= e^{-s\xi} \bigg[ \phi_0 + \bigg(-\frac{1}{2c\theta}\phi_0 - \frac{1}{2c(1-r)}\psi_- \bigg) \int_0^\xi e^{(s-1)\xi'} \ d\xi' \bigg] \\ &= e^{-s\xi} \bigg[ \phi_0 + \bigg(-\frac{1}{2c\theta}\phi_0 - \frac{1}{2c(1-r)}\psi_- \bigg)\frac{1}{s-1} (e^{(s-1)\xi} - 1) \bigg] \\ &= \bigg[ \phi_0 + \bigg(\frac{1}{2c\theta}\phi_0 + \frac{1}{2c(1-r)}\psi_- \bigg)\frac{1}{s-1}\bigg] e^{-s\xi} - \frac{1}{s-1}\bigg(\frac{1}{2c\theta}\phi_0 + \frac{1}{2c(1-r)}\psi_- \bigg)e^{-\xi} \end{align*} The initial convolution in the first step requires $\Re(r) < 1$ or $\psi_- = 0$. The later integral requires $s \ne 1$.

For $\xi < 0$ we have $$\begin{align*} \phi' + s\phi &= -\frac{1}{2c\theta}\phi_0 e^{\xi} - \frac{1}{2c}\psi_- \bigg(e^{-\xi} \int_{-\infty}^\xi e^{(1-r)\xi'} \ d\xi' + e^{\xi} \int_{\xi}^0 e^{-(1+r)\xi'} \ d\xi' \bigg] \\ &=-\frac{1}{2c\theta}\phi_0 e^{\xi} - \frac{1}{2c}\psi_- \bigg(\frac{1}{1-r}e^{-r\xi} + \frac{1}{-(1+r)}e^{\xi}\big(1 - e^{-(1+r)\xi}\big) \bigg) \\ &=-\frac{1}{2c\theta}\phi_0 e^{\xi} - \frac{1}{2c}\psi_- \bigg(\frac{1}{1-r}e^{-r\xi} - \frac{1}{1+r}e^{\xi} + \frac{1}{1+r} e^{-r\xi} \bigg) \\ &= \left(\frac{1}{2c(1+r)}\psi_- - \frac{1}{2c\theta}\phi_0\right) e^{\xi} - \frac{1}{c(1-r^2)}\psi_- e^{-r\xi} \\ \phi(\xi) &= e^{-s\xi} \bigg[ \phi_0 + \int_0^\xi e^{s\xi'} \bigg(\left(\frac{1}{2c(1+r)}\psi_- - \frac{1}{2c\theta}\phi_0\right) e^{\xi'} - \frac{1}{c(1-r^2)}\psi_- e^{-r\xi'} \bigg) \ d\xi' \\ &= e^{-s\xi} \bigg[ \phi_0 + \left(\frac{1}{2c(1+r)}\psi_- - \frac{1}{2c\theta}\phi_0\right) \int_0^\xi e^{(1+s)\xi'} \ d\xi' - \frac{1}{c(1-r^2)}\psi_- \int_0^\xi e^{(s-r)\xi'} \ d\xi' \bigg] \\ &= e^{-s\xi} \bigg[\phi_0 + \left(\frac{1}{2c(1+r)}\psi_- - \frac{1}{2c\theta}\phi_0\right)\frac{1}{1+s} \big(e^{(1+s)\xi} - 1\big) - \frac{1}{c(1-r^2)}\psi_-\frac{1}{s-r} \big(e^{(s-r)\xi} - 1\big) \bigg] \\ &= \phi_0e^{-s\xi} + \left(\frac{1}{2c(1+r)}\psi_- - \frac{1}{2c\theta}\phi_0\right)\frac{1}{1+s} \big(e^{\xi} - e^{-s\xi}\big) - \frac{1}{c(1-r^2)}\psi_-\frac{1}{s-r} \big(e^{-r\xi} - e^{-s\xi}\big) \\ &= \bigg(\phi_0 - \left(\frac{1}{2c(1+r)}\psi_- - \frac{1}{2c\theta}\phi_0\right)\frac{1}{1+s} + \frac{1}{c(1-r^2)}\psi_-\frac{1}{s-r} \bigg)e^{-s\xi} + \bigg( \left(\frac{1}{2c(1+r)}\psi_ - \frac{1}{2c\theta}\phi_0\right)\frac{1}{1+s} \bigg)e^{\xi} + \bigg(- \frac{1}{c(1-r^2)}\psi_- \frac{1}{s-r} \bigg) e^{-r\xi}. \end{align*}$$ As in the previous calculation, the initial integral requires $\Re(r) > 1$ for the convolution to converge. This guarantees that $r \ne -1$ thus this calculation of the convolution is applicable in all cases. The form of the later integrals requires $s \ne - 1$ and $s \ne r$. Note that $s \ne r$ is equivalent to $\alpha \gamma = \mu$ and does not depend on $\lambda$.

Case 1: $\psi_-, \psi_+ \ne 0$

Here, these two conditions impose restrictions on the exponential rates $r, r_+$. Specifically $$\begin{align*} 0 &< \Re(-r) & \Re(-r_+) &< 0\\ 0 &< \frac{1+\beta}{\alpha c} + \frac{1}{c} \Re(\lambda) & \frac{1}{\alpha c} + \frac{1}{c}\Re(\lambda) &<0\\ -\frac{1}{\alpha} - \frac{\beta}{\alpha} & < \Re(\lambda) & \Re(\lambda)&< -\frac{1}{\alpha}. \end{align*}$$

Thus, $$-\frac{1}{\alpha} - \frac{\beta}{\alpha} < \Re(\lambda) < -\frac{1}{\alpha}.$$

Case 1a

We have $\Re(s) < 0$ and $$ 0 = \phi_0 + \bigg(\frac{1}{2c\theta}\phi_0 + \frac{1}{2c(1-r)}\psi_- \bigg)\frac{1}{s-1}. $$ We are also bound by $\Re(r_+) > 0$ and $\Re(r) < 1$. This equation turns out not to be very restrictive. It can be written as the polynomial $$ 0 = \frac{\psi_-}{\phi_0} \theta + (1-r) + (s-1)(1-r)2c\theta. $$ Recall that $s$ and $r$ are afine functions of $\lambda$, so this is a quadratic in lambda, parameterized by the model parmeters, the choice of wave speed $c$, and the free variable $\frac{\psi_-}{\phi_0}$. Unfortunately, for any value of $\lambda$ there is a choice of $\frac{\psi_-}{\phi_0}$ that makes $\lambda$ a solution to this equation.

This gives any $\lambda \in \CC$ is an eigenvalue provided it satisfies the inequalities $$\begin{align*} \Re(s) < 0 & \iff \lambda > -\frac{1}{\mu} \\ \Re(r) < 1 & \iff \lambda > -c -\frac{1 + \beta}{\alpha} \\ \Re(r_+) > 0 & \iff \lambda < \frac{1}{\alpha}. \end{align*}$$ This is unfortunate because it implies there are eigenvalues with positive real part.

$\psi_+ = 0$ and $\Re(r) < 0$.

This case includes $\psi = Q'$, which we expect to correspond to $\lambda = 0$, thus we expect one solution corresponding to translation invariance. Hopefully, only one solution (a consequence of algebraic multiplicity being 1).

In this case, $\psi_- = \phi_- \frac{\beta}{c\alpha\theta}$ and the only unknown is $\lambda$. This makes it slightly different than case 1.

The restriction that $\Re(r) < 0$ also imposes that $\Re(s) < \frac{1 + \beta}{c \alpha} - \frac{1}{c \mu}$. Assuming $r \ne s$ (a separate case) we can determine the sign of $\Re(s)$ and thus restrict ourselves to one of the subcases listed in case 1. For large $\alpha$, we can take this expression on the right-hand-side of the inequality to be positive, and thus $\Re(s) > 0$.

$\psi_- = 0$ and $\Re(r_+) > 0$.

This simplifies the equations quite a bit. They reduce to a system of ODEs only coupled through $\phi_0$ which is arbitrary, so they are effectively decoupled.

We also have the restriction $\Re(s) > \frac{1}{c\alpha} - \frac{1}{c\mu}$ which is positive for large values of $\alpha$. The sign of $\Re(s)$ will force the coefficient of $e^{-s\xi}$ to be 0 in one of the pieces of the solution, thus giving $s$ implicitly as the solution to an algebraic equation. Solutions must be checked for consistency with the inequalities.

Notes:

This case looks like it might be easier. There is no dependence on $r$, so equations can be solved for $s$ rather than $\lambda$ which will make it easier to check for consistency.

$s = 1$.

This case fixes $s$, and as a consequence, also fixes $r$ and $r_+$. The form of the forcing term is slightly different, and must be handled separately. This should be straightforward. I expect the solution to be unbounded, and hence for this to not lead to eigen solutions.

$s = r$.

This only occurs under parameters that satisfy $(1+\beta)\mu = \alpha$. I believe it may be related to the bifurcation points, but I'm not sure. I'm not sure what to expect in this case.
