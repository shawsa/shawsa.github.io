We apply the new wave response differential equation to the problem of apparent motion.

Apparent Motion - Simple Case

We choose our stimulus to be of the form $$ I_u(x,t) = I_{(y^* - \Delta, y^*)}\left(x - \left\lfloor \frac{t}{T} \right\rfloor *T\Delta_c\right) $$ where $T$ is the constant amount of time between shifts of the apparent motion stimulus, and $T\Delta_c$ is the distance moved. We will first restrict ourselves to the time interval $t \in (0, T)$. Then, our stimulus in characteristic coordinates is given by $$ I_u(\xi, t) = I_{(y^* - \Delta - ct, y^* - ct)}(\xi). $$ In the simplest case, we will assume that the threshold crossing remains in the interior of the stimulus region for this time interval.

We substitute into the differential equation $$\begin{align*} D\nu' &= \langle v_1, I_u(\xi + \varepsilon \nu, t) \rangle \\ &= \langle v_1 , I_{(y^* - \Delta - ct - \varepsilon \nu, y^* - ct - \varepsilon \nu)}(\xi) \rangle. \end{align*}$$

Assuming that the effect from the back of the pulse is negligible, we then have $$\begin{align*} D\nu' &= \int_0^{y^* - \varepsilon \nu - ct} e^{-\xi/c\mu} \ d\xi \\ &= c\mu \big( 1 - e^{y^*}e^{-\varepsilon \nu - ct} \big). \end{align*}$$ We make the change of variables $y = \varepsilon \nu - ct$ and have $$\begin{align*} D \frac{y' - c}{\varepsilon} &= c\mu \big( 1 - e^{y^*}e^{-y} \big) \\ y' &= \underbrace{c + \frac{\varepsilon c \mu}{D}}_{a} - \underbrace{\frac{\varepsilon}{D}e^{y^*} }_{b}e^{-y} \\ y' &= a - be^{-y}. \end{align*}$$

We make the $u$-substitution $u = e^y$ and $u' = y'u$ and substitute $$\begin{align*} \frac{u'}{u} &= a - \frac{b}{u} \\ u' &= au - b \\ u' - au &= -b \\ \left[u e^{-at} \right]' &= -be^{-at} \\ ue^{-at} &= u_0 + \int_0^t -be^{-a\tau} \ d\tau \\ &= u_0 - \frac{b}{a}\left(1 - e^{-at} \right) \\ u(t) &= \frac{b}{a} + \left(u_0 - \frac{b}{a}\right)e^{at} \\ y(t) &= \log \left[ \frac{b}{a} + \left(e^{y_0} - \frac{b}{a}\right)e^{at} \right]. \end{align*}$$

We define the mapping $f(y_0) = y(T)-\Delta_c T$ and are interested in stable fixed points.
