Moving Least Squares Approximation

Moving least squares (MLS) is a modification of weighted least squares regressions where the weighting moves with the evaluation point. To begin, a radial kernel is chosen, idealy with compact support such as $$ \phi(r) = 1-\left(\frac{r}{\delta}\right)**2 $$ which has a support of $[0, \delta)$ for some chosen parameter $\delta$.

For a given set of data $\{(\vec{x}_i, f_i)\}_{i=1}^N$, we approximate $f$ at the point $\vec{y}$ by evaluating the polynomial $P_d(\vec{x}) \big|_{\vec{x}=\vec{y}}$ where $P_d(\vec{x})$ is a $d$-degree polynomial chosen to minimize $$ \sum_{n=1}^N (P_d(\vec{x}_i) - f_i)^2 \phi(|| \vec{x_i} - \vec{x}||), $$ in effect: weighted least squares under the weighting $W(\vec{y}) = \phi(||\vec{y} - \vec{x}||)$.

This produces a continuous interpolant that has the same smoothness as the kernel $\phi$. It may not interpolate, but it will converge to the function as $N \to \infty$ while varying $\delta$ to keep approximately the same number of points in the support of $\phi(||\vec{x} - \vec{y}||)$.
